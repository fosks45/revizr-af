# Revizr — Persona Pack

Six personas derived from UK student and parent distribution data, market research
findings, and the confirmed product scope (11+, KS3, GCSE, A-level; England, NI,
Wales; student–parent account pairs; B2B school channel signal).

Stereotypes have been actively rejected. Each persona is drawn from credible UK
demographic contexts and reflects the range of income, geography, exam level, and
family structure present in the real user population.

---

## Amara Osei-Mensah — Student, age 10, 11+ candidate, Birmingham

- **Profile:**
  Amara is a Year 5 pupil at a state primary school in Handsworth, Birmingham. Her
  parents are second-generation Ghanaian-British; her father works in logistics
  management, her mother is a community nurse. The family has one other child (age 7).
  Amara is academically strong in English and verbal reasoning but struggles with
  non-verbal reasoning and spatial problems. Her parents are targeting two grammar
  schools in the West Midlands (King Edward VI group) for entry in September 2026.
  The family does not use a private tutor — the cost (typically £35–45/hr in
  Birmingham for 11+ specialists) is stretched across the household budget. Amara uses
  a shared family tablet at home and has limited independent screen time. She is
  motivated by gold stars and collecting achievements but becomes frustrated and
  anxious when she cannot see why she got a question wrong.

- **Goals:**
  - Understand which parts of 11+ papers she finds hard so she is not surprised on
    exam day.
  - Practise on questions that actually appear on real tests, not made-up examples.
  - Feel confident going in rather than panicked.

- **Frustrations:**
  - Free 11+ resources (Bond, CGP, exam board sample tests) are inconsistent in
    quality and don't explain why an answer is wrong.
  - Her parents cannot identify which question types are weakening her overall score.
  - She finds generic timed papers stressful because they include topics she has
    already mastered mixed with ones she has not.

- **Device:** Shared family Android tablet (7-inch). Occasionally a school Chromebook
  during a permitted session. No personal smartphone.

- **Technical proficiency:** Low-to-moderate independent. Comfortable navigating
  apps but needs a clear, simple interface with no assumed tech literacy. Relies on
  an adult to set up accounts and configure settings.

- **JTBDs:**
  1. When I sit down to practise 11+ papers, I want to be shown only the question
     types I find difficult, so I can improve my weak areas without wasting time on
     questions I can already do.
  2. When I get a question wrong, I want to understand the correct method step by
     step, so I can learn from mistakes rather than just seeing the right answer.
  3. When I feel anxious before an exam, I want to see that I have already practised
     questions like these and improved, so I can feel prepared and calm rather than
     overwhelmed.

## Priya Sharma — Parent, age 42, primary-school parent, 11+ context, Leicester

- **Profile:**
  Priya lives in a semi-detached house in Oadby, Leicestershire — one of the highest-
  density areas in England for 11+ preparation pressure (grammar school competition
  is intense in the East Midlands). She is a part-time pharmacist and the primary
  educational decision-maker for her two children: Aaryan (age 10, Year 5, preparing
  for 11+) and Diya (age 7). Her husband, Rajan, is an accountant. The family
  currently pays £40/hr for an 11+ tutor for Aaryan, one session per week. Priya
  manages Aaryan's revision schedule herself (evenings after her pharmacy shift),
  using a mix of Bond assessment papers, CGP workbooks, and YouTube tutorials. She
  is on three local grammar school preparation Facebook groups and is an active
  Mumsnet reader. She is digitally comfortable — uses banking apps, ordering apps,
  and WhatsApp groups — but has never used an EdTech subscription platform beyond
  BBC Bitesize.

- **Goals:**
  - Know definitively whether Aaryan's tutoring sessions are producing improvement,
    not just whether he completed homework.
  - Identify the specific non-verbal reasoning and mathematics sub-topics where
    Aaryan is losing marks.
  - Reduce family stress at exam time by feeling that preparation has been systematic
    and evidence-based.

- **Frustrations:**
  - The tutor gives verbal feedback in sessions but Priya has no written record of
    Aaryan's topic-by-topic progress.
  - She cannot tell whether Aaryan is genuinely improving or just memorising the
    specific Bond papers she has already bought.
  - She has spent £280 on workbooks and papers this year, most of which Aaryan has
    already worked through, and she is running out of fresh authentic material.

- **Device:** iPhone 14 (primary). iPad at home. MacBook Air for work. All three
  are relevant touch points.

- **Technical proficiency:** High comfort with consumer apps. Willing to pay for
  software if the value proposition is clear. Has a Headspace subscription, a
  Deliveroo account, and Spotify — subscription-as-a-service is a known behaviour.

- **JTBDs:**
  1. When I am managing my child's 11+ preparation, I want to see which specific
     question types are costing him marks, so I can direct our limited practice time
     at the right topics.
  2. When my child completes a practice session, I want to receive a plain-English
     summary of what he worked on and how he performed, so I can have an informed
     conversation with him about progress without interrogating him.
  3. When I am evaluating whether Revizr is worth £20/month, I want to see a
     personalised diagnosis based on Aaryan's actual school report before I pay, so
     I can trust that the platform understands my child's situation rather than
     offering generic content.

## Jack Whitfield — Student, age 15, GCSE Year 10, Sheffield

- **Profile:**
  Jack is a Year 10 student at a comprehensive academy in Sheffield. He lives with
  his mum (single parent, works in NHS administration) and a younger brother. The
  household income is below the national median; there is no family budget for
  private tutoring. Jack is working towards GCSEs in nine subjects and is predicted
  grades averaging 5s and 6s. His weakest subjects are Chemistry and History. He is
  competitive — tracks his ranking within his friendship group on FIFA and on gaming
  leaderboards — but his attitude to formal revision is avoidant: he opens past
  papers, panics at the breadth of content, and closes them. He has a smartphone
  (his primary device for everything) and uses YouTube for entertainment but has
  never deliberately used it for revision. He occasionally visits exam board websites
  for past papers but typically downloads the whole paper and abandons it after two
  questions. He is not motivated by academic praise from adults but is strongly
  motivated by visible personal progress and avoiding embarrassment in front of peers.

- **Goals:**
  - Know exactly which Chemistry and History topics to revise without having to read
    through entire specifications.
  - Make revision feel like it is doing something useful — he wants to see scores
    going up, not just complete a checklist.
  - Be adequately prepared so results day does not come as a shock to him or his mum.

- **Frustrations:**
  - Whole past papers are overwhelming — he does not know where to start.
  - Generic revision apps feel childish or are full of advertising.
  - He has no one at home who can advise him on which Chemistry topics matter most
    for AQA.

- **Device:** Android smartphone (primary, always with him). Home laptop occasionally.
  No tablet.

- **Technical proficiency:** High comfort with apps, social platforms, and
  subscription services (free tiers of Spotify, YouTube). Cynical about educational
  software; influenced by peer recommendation.

- **JTBDs:**
  1. When I sit down to revise and do not know where to start, I want to be told
     exactly which topics to focus on for my specific exam board, so I can start
     practising immediately without reading through the entire specification.
  2. When I complete a set of practice questions, I want to see my score improve
     over time across sessions, so I feel like revision is working and stay
     motivated to continue.
  3. When I am preparing for a Chemistry or History exam, I want to practise only
     on questions that have appeared in real AQA exams, so I understand the exact
     wording and format I will face on exam day.

## Siobhan O'Neill — Parent, age 38, secondary parent, GCSE context, Belfast

- **Profile:**
  Siobhan lives in the Lisburn Road area of Belfast. She is a secondary school parent
  with two children: Caitlin (age 15, Year 11, sitting GCSE examinations under CCEA
  — the Northern Ireland exam board) and Fionnán (age 11). Siobhan works part-time
  as a dental receptionist; her husband Eoin is a joiner. The family uses one private
  tutor for Maths (£35/hr, fortnightly) but cannot afford additional subject tutors.
  Caitlin is predicted Bs and Cs across most subjects but is significantly weak in
  Maths and Double Award Science (both CCEA). Siobhan is frustrated by the NI-specific
  exam board situation: almost all revision apps she has found are AQA or Edexcel
  focused, and content rarely aligns with CCEA specifications. She is motivated,
  organised, and technology-literate (uses Amazon, online banking, NHS app) but
  deeply sceptical of apps that claim to cover Northern Ireland and then don't.

- **Goals:**
  - Find revision support for Caitlin that actually covers CCEA content, not just
    English boards.
  - Have a clear picture of whether Caitlin's Maths is improving between fortnightly
    tutor sessions.
  - Reduce the family's anxiety about GCSE results by having a structured, visible
    preparation system.

- **Frustrations:**
  - Nearly every EdTech product she has tried claims to cover "all UK boards" but
    either has no CCEA content or has clearly not been maintained.
  - She has no visibility into what happens in the fortnight between Maths tutoring
    sessions.
  - Caitlin independently claims she has been revising but Siobhan cannot verify
    this.

- **Device:** Samsung Galaxy smartphone. Windows laptop at home. Chrome is her
  primary browser.

- **Technical proficiency:** Moderate-to-high. Comfortable with subscriptions and
  apps. Has a Netflix account and uses an NHS health app. Would not be put off by
  a monthly subscription if the value is real.

- **JTBDs:**
  1. When Caitlin says she has been revising, I want to see a session log with the
     actual time spent and questions attempted, so I can know whether revision
     happened and hold an honest conversation with her.
  2. When I am looking for GCSE revision support in Northern Ireland, I want to
     find a platform that genuinely covers CCEA past paper content, so Caitlin
     practises on questions that match her actual examinations.
  3. When I review Caitlin's progress each week, I want a plain-English summary of
     whether she is on track for her target grade, so I can decide whether to
     increase tutoring frequency or whether she is managing the material.

## Tariq Hassan — Student, age 17, A-level Year 12, Manchester

- **Profile:**
  Tariq is a Year 12 student at a sixth-form college in Salford, studying A-level
  Biology, Chemistry, and Mathematics (AQA and Edexcel boards). He is applying to
  read Medicine at university and knows that his A-level grades are binary for
  his ambition — he needs AAA minimum. His parents (his father runs a convenience
  store, his mother is a teaching assistant) are supportive but cannot provide
  substantive academic help at A-level standard. Tariq is self-motivated but
  organised in bursts — he works intensively for two weeks before tests and then
  slacks off. He is aware that this approach is risky for A-level and is looking
  for a system that keeps him consistently on track rather than cramming. He has
  previously used Seneca Learning (GCSE) and thought it was useful but found the
  A-level content too thin on authentic past paper practice. He is comfortable
  spending money on study tools if he can see they are effective — he bought a
  premium revision guide set (£45 total).

- **Goals:**
  - Identify which specific A-level topics across Biology, Chemistry, and Maths are
    genuinely unresolved rather than just feeling like they are.
  - Build a consistent daily revision habit rather than panic-driven cycles.
  - Have access to a deep pool of authentic A-level past paper questions that tests
    his knowledge at exam standard, not dumbed-down quizzes.

- **Frustrations:**
  - Most revision apps do not have sufficient A-level depth — they are evidently
    designed for GCSE and have thin A-level content as an afterthought.
  - He knows there are past papers on the AQA and Edexcel websites but they are not
    organised by topic, so he still has to search manually for questions on, say,
    "enzyme kinetics" or "integration by parts."
  - His college teachers do not have time to give him personalised feedback on which
    topics need the most attention between formal assessments.

- **Device:** MacBook Air (primary for study). iPhone 15 (secondary, used for quick
  revision on the bus or between college sessions). Strong preference for cross-device
  consistency.

- **Technical proficiency:** High. Uses GitHub, Google Drive, Notion. Is a careful
  consumer of digital tools — does not install apps he does not trust or use. Will
  read reviews, check Reddit, and consider value for money before subscribing.

- **JTBDs:**
  1. When I am preparing for an A-level exam, I want to practise questions drawn
     specifically from the topics the AI has identified as my weak areas, so I spend
     revision time where it will make the most difference to my grade.
  2. When I start a revision session, I want to see my personalised question pack
     ready to go without having to search exam board websites for topic-specific
     past paper questions, so I can begin focused practice immediately.
  3. When I complete a question, I want to see the official mark scheme explanation
     alongside my response, so I understand the examiner's expectations and close
     the gap between my answers and full-marks responses.

## Ms Denise Okafor — Secondary Schoolteacher / Tutor, age 34, GCSE/A-level, London

- **Profile:**
  Denise teaches GCSE and A-level English Literature and Language at a mixed
  comprehensive in Walthamstow, East London. She also tutors privately two evenings
  per week (4 students, £45/hr, AQA English). She is Head of Year 10 in addition to
  her teaching duties. She is aware of the gap between what students are told to
  revise and what they actually do between lessons. She uses Seneca Learning for
  class homework setting because it is free and the dashboard shows completion status,
  but she finds it imprecise — she cannot tell which specific question types within
  a topic a student is struggling with. She is interested in platforms that her
  private tutoring students could use between sessions to practise on authentic
  questions aligned to exactly the sub-topics she has identified as weak. She has
  heard of Revizr through a Twitter/X thread about EdTech and is curious but not yet
  convinced. She would be an influential recommender if the product earns her trust.
  As a teacher she has institutional constraints: any platform she recommends through
  the school requires approval from the Head of ICT and potentially a DPO sign-off
  on data handling for student accounts.

- **Goals:**
  - Find a revision tool she can recommend to tutoring students that continues the
    targeted work she does in sessions, using real AQA English past paper questions.
  - Have enough visibility into a student's between-session progress to make tutoring
    sessions more efficient (picking up where the platform identified new weaknesses,
    rather than general review).
  - Identify a school-approvable platform with robust child data handling that she
    can present to the Head of ICT.

- **Frustrations:**
  - Seneca is generic — it is not targeted at the exact sub-topics she has told
    students to revise.
  - She cannot easily generate a custom question set from authentic AQA English papers
    on any existing platform.
  - The time she spends in tutoring sessions re-explaining concepts the student should
    have consolidated between sessions is time not spent moving forward.

- **Device:** School Windows laptop (daytime). Personal MacBook Pro (evenings).
  iPhone 13.

- **Technical proficiency:** High. Uses Google Classroom, Seneca, Turnitin. Evaluates
  EdTech with a critical eye — cares about data privacy, exam board alignment, and
  whether the claimed functionality is real.

- **JTBDs:**
  1. When I assign between-session revision to a tutoring student, I want to see
     a report of which authentic AQA English questions they attempted and how they
     performed, so I can begin the next session at the point of actual difficulty
     rather than reviewing material they already know.
  2. When I recommend a revision platform to a student or parent, I want confidence
     that the questions are drawn from real AQA past papers, so my professional
     credibility is not compromised by pointing them to inaccurate or off-spec
     content.
  3. When I am considering recommending a platform to students at my school, I want
     to review the data handling and privacy policies for under-18 students, so I
     can satisfy myself and the school's DPO that the platform meets UK data
     protection requirements.
