# Competitor Intelligence Matrix — Revizr (002-revizr)

## Coverage

Twelve products are assessed. Threat level is scored 1–5 where:
- **5 — Critical threat:** well-funded, large user base, directly overlapping
  product, active in Revizr's exact market segment.
- **4 — High threat:** strong position in adjacent segment, could extend into
  Revizr's lane with moderate effort.
- **3 — Moderate threat:** significant users but limited overlap or structural
  weaknesses that reduce head-to-head competition.
- **2 — Low threat:** different market positioning, different buyer, or structural
  moat (e.g., free / public sector) that prevents direct revenue competition.
- **1 — Negligible:** operates in overlapping space but so different in approach
  or scale that competitive impact is minimal.

## Revizr Differentiation Argument per Competitor

### vs Seneca Learning (Threat 5)

Seneca is the highest-threat competitor and the most important to monitor. Seneca's
free tier makes it the default starting point for many GCSE students. Revizr's
differentiation is threefold:

1. **Authentic past paper content.** Seneca's questions are synthesised or licensed —
   they are curriculum-aligned but not real exam questions. Revizr's 30,000+ paper
   database delivers questions that have appeared on actual exams. UK students and
   parents consistently express preference for "real" past paper practice; this is
   the primary tutoring industry's content standard. Seneca cannot replicate this
   database at speed.

2. **AI weakness diagnosis from school reports.** Seneca personalises based on
   in-app performance only. Revizr diagnoses weaknesses from external evidence
   (school reports, teacher notes) before the student has answered a single
   in-app question. This is a qualitatively different entry point — it means
   the first session is already targeted, not generically adaptive.

3. **Parent dashboard with session-level visibility.** Seneca has basic parent
   features but not the real-time session log, score progression, and plain-English
   summary that Revizr's parent dashboard provides. The parent job-to-be-done is
   underserved by Seneca.

**Competitive exposure:** Seneca could build a past paper module if it licensed
content from exam boards. Monitor for any licensing announcements. Revizr should
establish and communicate the authentic-paper brand early.

---

### vs Tassomai (Threat 3)

Tassomai is the most directly comparable paid subscription UK EdTech product, but
is primarily a Science platform. Revizr's cross-subject breadth (all GCSE and
A-level subjects on the paper database) is the main differentiator. Revizr should
target Tassomai's non-science subject gaps actively in SEO and content marketing.

---

### vs Atom Learning (Threat 4 in 11+ segment)

Atom Learning holds strong brand awareness in the 11+ preparation segment — exactly
one of Revizr's target levels. Atom uses publisher-generated content; Revizr's
authentic 11+ past papers (including real grammar school and independent school
papers) are structurally superior for exam readiness. Revizr should position its
11+ offering explicitly against Atom on the authenticity of content. Atom's pricing
(£30/month) validates that parents pay at this level for 11+ preparation.

---

### vs Educake (Threat 2)

Educake is school-facing and teacher-dependent. It is not a direct consumer
competitor. It is relevant as a school-channel comparator: when Revizr develops its
school partnership model (Year 2), Educake's school pricing (~£200–500/year/school)
is a useful benchmark. Revizr should price school licences at a premium to Educake
given the past-paper content quality.

---

### vs Isaac Physics / BBC Bitesize (Threat 2)

Both are free and not revenue competitors. Their significance is that they anchor
the user expectation of free revision content. Revizr's response: communicate that
free past papers exist on exam board websites (they do), but that the value of
Revizr is not the papers — it is the AI-driven targeting, the personalised pack
assembly, and the parent dashboard. The product must be able to articulate why
£20/month is worth paying when GCSE revision notes are free. This is a marketing
challenge, not a product deficiency.

---

### vs GCSEPod (Threat 3)

GCSEPod is video-first and school-distributed. Revizr is question-first and
consumer-distributed. The products are complementary rather than directly competing.
Revizr could position itself as the practice layer that works alongside GCSEPod's
explanation layer. A school partnership that positions Revizr and GCSEPod as
complementary tools is plausible. Revizr does not need to win the video-revision
market; it needs to own the targeted-practice market.

---

### vs Pearson Revise / CGP (Threat 3 — brand perception)

Print revision guides are not software competitors but they are the parent's mental
model of "revision support." Revizr should acknowledge this reference point in
marketing: "Better than a revision guide — it knows which chapters your child
actually needs." The print guide brands set quality expectations; Revizr exceeds
them on personalisation. Price comparison: a full set of GCSE revision guides across
8 subjects costs £40–64 (a one-time purchase but reused across siblings). Revizr at
£180–300/year is more expensive per year than print, but delivers personalisation
and parent visibility that print cannot. Annual ROI framing is essential.

Revizr's target quadrant — high quality authentic content, high personalisation,
mid-high price — is occupied by no current mainstream competitor. Atom Learning is
in this quadrant but only for the 11+ segment. Seneca is close but on the
personalisation axis is weaker (no school-report AI diagnosis) and on content
quality is weaker (no authentic papers). This gap is real and defensible.
